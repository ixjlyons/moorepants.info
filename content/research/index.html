--- title: Research subtitle: asking the right questions description: moorepants' ponderings extends: portfolio_listing.j2 default_block: test listable: false --- {% filter restructuredtext %} Research Statement ================== If one word can represent the glue in my research ideas and intentions it would be "human". With a mechanical engineering focus, I'm mostly concerned with various ways humans interact with machines. The past decades of mechanical engineering advancement have brought us great strides in automatic control and machine design. But this body of research often neglects that humans are still and will be for the foreseeable future an integrable part of machines. Machines will need to become more and more aware of the human's actions and intentions, complementing the higher cerebral control that computers cannot yet demonstrate with the massive data parsing and repetitive tasks that computers excel at. We must understand the human to better interact with its environment which is starting to include more advanced vehicles, environments, machines, and software. The more we understand how the human works the better these devices can be designed to complement the human's strengths. My doctoral dissertation work focused on the human's unique ability to balance a mostly unstable vehicle, the bicycle. The bicycle control problem is an ideal platform for investigating many, if not all of the facets of complex human control and complementary machine design, all in a tractable, economically viable, and familiar machine. On-board data acquisition has improved dramatically with the advent of tiny sensor and data collection abilities that are being pushed by the smartphone industry, which is helping propel engineering into a big data field. This sensor and data collection allowed me to collect substantial amounts of data from both the bicycle and the human subject in maneuvering experiments. I made use of a combination of first principles modeling techniques and grey box system identification approaches to develop realistic models of both the bicycle and the human's control behavior. This research is covered in detail in my `dissertation `_ and a series of journal and conference papers found in my resume. My doctoral work has built my aptitude in a broad range of theoretical and experimental sub-disciplines which include: - Multibody dynamics and classical mechanics - Classical and modern automatic control systems - System identification - Signal processing - Human biomechanics - Human operator control (i.e. manual control) - Vehicle dynamics (single track vehicles, automobiles, and aircraft) - Aircraft dynamics and control - Vehicle handling qualities - Open source software engineering and agile development - Statistical modeling - Machine design - Sports engineering - Appropriate technology - Human powered machines - Wind tunnel experimentation - Mobile instrumentation My future research directions are going to leverage collaborative initiatives to fill in the gaps in my expertise with the intent of developing a more holistic research plan centered around the human as a controller and his or her interactions with machines. In particular, connection to the fields of cognitive psychology, neuroscience, and machine learning will be a priority. The prediction of human behavior in a machine interaction sense requires greater understanding of the mind and the complexities of the human's internal control scheme. My primary goal will be do assemble a team of scientists (students, post docs, and fellow collegues) to tackle research fundamental concepts in our understanding of the human's control system and the machines that the human interacts with. The applications are wide ranged but the research will primarily focus on the basics of human motor control from simple balance to manipulator control to vehicle control. I am interested in pushing the boundaries into biofeeback in automatic control systems and operator "awareness". In my tenure as a graduate student researcher I have already gained a great deal of experience in project management, student mentoring, and successful grant writing. The final years of my graduate work was funded by an National Science Foundation grant, of which I played a large role in writing and managing under my principal investigators. Combining this with successful grants from the Department of State (Fulbright), many local UC Davis grants, and two Google Summer of Code grants, I feel quite prepared to secure funding and management a large lab and several research projects. When does the automatic take control of the vehicle if the human isn't up to the control task? Can automatic control constantly be aware of the human's state such that it can adapt to the human's ability or inability? Can machines become aware of their surroundings such that they never harm a human? Autmoatic control of manually controlled systems must be able to augment the human's actions both enhacing their ability and filling in when the human loses the ability to be an adequate controller. It should be aware of things the human cannot be. Research Proposals ================== The following give brief proposals for research projects ideas and seeds. Manual Control and Human Machine Interaction -------------------------------------------- I'd like to explore the human as a machine controller for very fundamental problems. For example, how do we balance a broom stick on our hand? What is the fundamental sensory information that we take in, how we process that, and produce the correct control action. Developing experiments around simple input/output relationships with machines and software can help expose the essense of human control. In constract to simple systems and first principles modeling cosntructs, the collection of a large amount of varying dynamic data from forces and kinematics to psyiological sensations combined with machine learning and system identication will allow for the development of predictive models of human control behavior in complex tasks. Cheap UAVs offer a new complex platform for testing helicopter dynamics. Balance ------- One of the most fundamental control tasks we do as a human is to balance. Balancing is learned at a very young age when it is a task that dominates the frontal cortex computations but which becomes a learned skill which moves to the spinal cord. I'd like to study details of postrual control and the way in which we learn the skill through carefully crafted experiments that can expose the sensory motor control the human uses in balancing. Single Track Vehicle Dynamics ----------------------------- Single track vehicles such as bicycles, motorcycles, unicycles offer a unique platform to study and understand human control. These vehicles limit the human's input sources to affect the vehicles behavior which allow for more controlled input/output experimental reslationsips t be studied. Furthermore the vehicles themselves have non-trivial open loop dynamics which provides a rich system for exposing the human's unique control abilities such as non-minimum phase, instabilty, and directional control. Vehicle and Machine Handling Qualities -------------------------------------- When human's interact with machines whether it be flying an aircraft or playing a video game there is feel, a subjective opinion on the ease of control. Does the machine do what I want it to with minimal effort? The performance while completing a task with a manually control machine and the machines open loop dynamics can be objectively measured. There must be connections between the the subjective feeling and the objective measurements. Unlocking this connection will allow for the design of machines that compliment the human in a passive way and in combination with automatic control will allow machines to adapt the machine's dynamics in response to the humans desires. But understanding what makes a machine easy to control will take an interdiciplianry approach that involves an understanding of dynamics, control, neuroscience, and pyschology. Vehicle Model Software and Database ----------------------------------- Scientists create models of vehicles constantly. There are well known models like the "bicycle" model for cars, the Pacjeka automobile tire model, the Whipple bicycle model, the Sharp motorcycle model, etc. The currently availabilty of free an unhindered use of these models detracts from the scientific community being able to trust resutls. A large database that allowed models to developed, submitted and validated for easy reuse will. I'd like to start with vehicle models, but this concept can extend to any system described by differential equations and such. Vehicle Simulation Database --------------------------- Dynamics data is constantly collected on different vehilcles but is only stored locally on scientists computers. If there were an easy way to stored time series measurements with accompanying meta data, a large database of simulations could be built up. This would allow modelers to validate their models against thousands and thousands of simulations. In my field, most models are accepted after being validated on only a tiny set of data. Imagine if you could validate your model on huge datasets of simulations. Bicycle Tire Modeling --------------------- The rigid body dynamics bicycles and motorcycles have advanced enough for most prediction abiliaties, but the most glaring deficiency is models that can accurately predict the ground reaction forces between a tire and the surface that it is rolling on. I'm interested in creating large amounts of tire force data and identifiying models for validation and creation of first principles models. Open Source Software -------------------- The academic needs powerful cutting edges computation tools at his or her disposal. In academia, I believe that these tools are best provided by open source software that is collaboratively developed among the leading scientists in the field. The nature of open source software parallels the idea of reproducible, reusable science. I've spent a great deal of time working on open source software as a tightly knit companion to my research. All of my research will have open source software as an integral component and here are some specific examples of projects: - Dynamics (problem development, simulation, and visualization) - Symbolic equation of motion generation for dynamic systems - Control Systems - System Identification I'm interested in developing reusale, tested open source software suites that leverage cloud computing resources to solve big problems in my field, but to have the software general enough that many other fields will use the software.
