--- title: Human Control of a Bicycle subtitle: what about bears? description: what I studied for my phd degree startdate: !!timestamp '2003-06-01 10:00:00' enddate: !!timestamp '2003-12-31 10:00:00' --- {% mark image -%} {% filter restructuredtext %} .. image:: {{ media_url('images/jason-on-instrumented-bicycle.jpg') }} :class: img-rounded {% endfilter %} {%- endmark %} {% mark excerpt %} {% filter restructuredtext %} My graduate work at UC Davis was rounded out with a project that focused on identifying the control actions and model employed by a human during simple manuevers. I constructed a heavily instrumented bicycle and collected a great deal of data during a series of experiments in which the rider attempted to balance a bicycle under lateral perturbations. This data was then used to identify both a plant and control model of the underlying system and validate our hypostheses about the rider's control structure. The work is detailed both in `my dissertation`_ and `my lab's website`_. .. _my dissertation: http://moorepants.github.com/dissertation .. _my lab's website: http://biosport.ucdavis.edu/research-projects/bicycle {% endfilter %} {% endmark %} {% filter restructuredtext %} Videos ------ These are two videos that were produced about the research: {% endfilter %} Future Idea Snippets -------------------- - Use a Kalman filter to generate the angles, rates, and accelerations of the system and then run the M, C, K linear least squares fitting routine to data. This may give more consistentcy between the angles, rates, and accels and lower the uncertainity in the identified parameters in the plant id. - Setup up an optimization problem that maximizes our analytic handling quality metric with respect to the bicycle's physical parameters. If lucky, this could spit out some unusual type of optimal vehicle. This concept leads to deriving vehicle designs from optimization instead of human intuition and experience.
